### Deliverable 2 — AI Governance Policy Papers (2 documents)
Two GA-ready policy papers providing the analytical and governance framework for AI policy design in the cultural and creative economies of the 10 pledge countries.

- **Paper 1:** *Structural Impact Assessment of Bilateral AI Governance Frameworks on Developing Economies* — examining how emerging bilateral AI access architectures affect digital sovereignty, innovation, and cultural production in LAC.
- **Paper 2:** *Pax Chromata: A Practitioner Evidence Integration Protocol for LAC AI Governance* — a model for how developing-economy stakeholders can systematically contribute practitioner evidence to ITU, OECD, and UN AI governance processes.

**Deadline:** December 1, 2026  
**Audience:** UN General Assembly, WTO, ITU, UNESCO, IDB, CAF, bilateral ministries

### Deliverable 3 — AI Kernel Protocol (This Repository)
The AI Kernel Protocol (AKP) is the technical infrastructure deliverable of this pledge. It is an open specification for provider-agnostic AI intelligence infrastructure, designed to enable organizations in developing countries to deploy sovereign, resilient AI systems without dependency on any single provider's commercial, political, or jurisdictional decisions.

**Status:** Specification published. Reference implementation in production.  
**Access:** Open specification (this repository). Reference implementation: Avant.Dev proprietary.  
**Distribution:** Freely available for implementation under AKP License v1.0.

### Deliverable 4 — Policy Navigator
The Policy Navigator is an AI-powered policy intelligence tool, accessible at [pn.avant.dev](https://pn.avant.dev), providing real-time monitoring and analysis of AI governance processes relevant to LAC cultural economies.

**Status:** In development. Co-development interest confirmed: World Bank Group Outcomes Department (June 24, 2026).  
**Function:** Reduce the information asymmetry that prevents LAC practitioners from participating meaningfully in ITU, OECD, and UN AI governance processes.

## Why the AI Kernel Protocol Is a Policy Instrument

The AKP was designed to solve a technical problem. But technical architectures carry political consequences.

When a single jurisdiction can suspend frontier AI model access for all foreign nationals globally — without multilateral recourse, without due process, and without governance architecture designed to represent affected communities — the technology stack itself becomes a vector of dependence.

Provider agnosticism is, in this context, a form of sovereignty. The ability to migrate from one AI provider to another — without rewriting systems, without renegotiating access, without diplomatic intermediation — is infrastructure resilience for the AI era.

The 10 countries of this pledge operate in contexts where:

- Frontier AI model access can be revoked by bilateral decisions they had no role in making
- AI governance standards are being shaped by technical communities in which fewer than 5% of participating private entities are from developing economies
- Cultural and creative industries — which represent 3–7% of LAC GDP — remain almost entirely absent from AI governance processes despite their direct exposure to AI-mediated risks

AKP is the technical layer that addresses the first condition. The policy papers address the second. The Policy Navigator addresses the third.

Together, they form an integrated response to the governance gap this pledge was designed to fill.

## Addendum: Faith and Commitment to the Human Condition

This pledge began as a technical gesture — one ICT SME from Mexico City, registering a commitment to a UN digital coalition. It has become something larger: a statement about how the world we want to build is different from the world currently being constructed without us.

The communities in Argentina, Barbados, Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Guatemala, Jamaica, Mexico, Panama, and Venezuela that this pledge serves are not passive. They are musicians who use AI to master recordings. Artists who use generative tools to expand their visual languages. Writers who use language models in their mother tongue. Programmers who build on platforms whose terms of service were written in legal systems foreign to theirs. Entrepreneurs who depend on cloud infrastructure they cannot audit, governed by frameworks they cannot influence.

These are not marginal conditions. This is the condition of most of humanity.

The AI Kernel Protocol is, at its technical layer, a specification. But at its human layer, it is a refusal. A refusal to accept that the architecture of AI must replicate the architecture of dependence. A refusal to accept that provider agnosticism is only a developer preference and not a development right.

The pledge we make here — to these 10 countries, to the ITU, to the multilateral institutions whose mandate includes the communities we represent — is not a pledge to attend conferences or produce reports. Those are instruments. The pledge is this:

**We will build the infrastructure that makes it possible for the practitioners of the Global South to participate in — not merely consume — the artificial intelligence systems and governance frameworks that will define the coming century.**

We believe this is possible. We believe it is necessary. We believe it is, in the plainest sense, the right thing to do.

This is what the AI Kernel Protocol is for.
